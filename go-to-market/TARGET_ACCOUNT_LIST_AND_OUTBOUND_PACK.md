# TierZero Target Account List and Outbound Pack

## Purpose
This pack defines who to target first for the **Service Desk Zero** pilot and how to open the conversation without drifting into vague AI platform talk.

The offer stays narrow:
- overnight service desk queue clearance
- first workflow: password reset / account unlock for standard employee accounts
- 30-45 day pilot
- measured on queue reduction, speed, and trust

## What a good first account looks like
Target accounts where all of this is true:
- 200-5000 employees
- internal IT service desk with a meaningful standard employee population
- ServiceNow or a similarly mature ticket workflow already in place
- repetitive password reset / unlock volume
- overnight or early-morning backlog pain
- clear audit, policy, or approval requirements
- at least one adjacent workflow to expand into after proof

If the account cannot produce repetitive ticket volume, backlog pain, and a believable expansion path, skip it.

## First account archetypes

### Archetype 1 - Multi-shift operators with daytime IT coverage
Best fit because the business runs beyond normal support hours, but the service desk does not.

Examples:
- healthcare provider groups and hospital systems
- logistics and distribution companies
- manufacturers with multiple shifts
- airlines, travel ops, and field-service-heavy businesses

Why they fit:
- users get locked out outside core IT hours
- morning queue pain is visible and expensive
- standard employee account volume is high
- one repetitive workflow can create an obvious win fast

### Archetype 2 - Regulated mid-market companies with audit pressure
Best fit because they care about control and reviewability, not just speed.

Examples:
- regional banks and credit unions
- insurers
- healthcare administration companies
- regulated business services firms

Why they fit:
- repetitive access work still exists
- audit trail and policy boundaries matter
- "safe automation" is easier to sell than broad autonomy
- clean escalation behavior is a strong differentiator

### Archetype 3 - Multi-site companies with centralized IT and lean support teams
Best fit because a small central service desk gets hammered by repetitive employee support work.

Examples:
- retail chains with corporate IT
- franchise or branch-based businesses
- PE-backed roll-ups
- companies that recently merged systems or locations

Why they fit:
- user volume is spread across many sites
- support load piles up overnight or across time zones
- password resets and account unlocks are routine and low-value
- leadership already wants leverage without adding headcount

### Archetype 4 - Shared-services organizations with high ticket repetition
Best fit because they already think in queue terms and care about throughput.

Examples:
- BPOs and contact centers
- large internal shared-services teams
- business services firms with centralized employee support

Why they fit:
- repetitive ticket classes are easy to spot
- queue reduction language lands better than "AI transformation"
- expansion into adjacent fulfillment workflows is usually obvious

## Who to contact inside each account

### Operational owner
Primary target. This person feels the queue pain directly.

Titles to look for:
- Head of Service Desk
- Director of Service Desk
- Director of End User Support
- IT Support Manager
- Director of IT Operations

What they care about:
- smaller morning backlog
- faster completion time
- fewer low-value tickets consuming analysts
- clean escalations instead of messy exceptions

### Executive sponsor
Needed for budget and pilot sponsorship.

Titles to look for:
- CIO
- VP of IT
- VP of Infrastructure and Operations
- Senior Director of IT Operations

What they care about:
- measurable queue reduction
- labor leverage without a risky platform bet
- pilot scope that feels controllable
- clear expansion path after proof

### Technical and risk reviewer
Bring in once interest exists.

Titles to look for:
- ServiceNow Platform Owner
- IAM Manager
- IT Compliance Lead
- Security Operations or Identity lead

What they care about:
- approved workflow boundaries
- audit trail quality
- escalation behavior
- no hidden automation outside policy

## Pain signals that qualify an account
Pursue accounts when you can confirm at least 4 of these 7 signals.

1. **ServiceNow is in place**
   - Public job posts, employee profiles, partner pages, or tooling pages reference ServiceNow.

2. **The business runs beyond one daytime shift**
   - 24/7 operations, multiple shifts, nationwide locations, or global user base.

3. **The service desk is visibly backlog-sensitive**
   - Job posts mention SLA pressure, backlog reduction, ticket volume, or queue ownership.

4. **Identity or access requests are a known pain area**
   - Password reset, unlock, access triage, or onboarding/offboarding support shows up in role descriptions or process docs.

5. **Audit and control matter**
   - Regulated industry, compliance language, or formal change / access processes.

6. **The team is lean relative to user footprint**
   - Small support org, recent cost controls, or signs the team is expected to do more with the same headcount.

7. **There is room to expand after the first workflow**
   - Access requests, software fulfillment, or other runbook-backed service desk work is clearly present.

## Disqualifiers
Do not spend early outbound on accounts where any of this is true:
- no clear ITSM workflow or no evidence of ServiceNow-like maturity
- fewer than roughly 200 employees or too little ticket volume
- the likely buyer only wants broad AI strategy talk
- password reset volume is already fully self-service and not a queue issue
- support work is mostly privileged, bespoke, or policy-ambiguous
- no believable expansion path beyond the first workflow

## Simple account scoring model
Score each target 0 or 1 on the seven signals above.

Prioritize like this:
- **6-7** = pursue now
- **4-5** = good second wave
- **0-3** = ignore for now

## First list build
Build the first outbound list as **40 accounts** total.

Suggested mix:
- 15 multi-shift operators
- 10 regulated mid-market accounts
- 10 multi-site centralized IT accounts
- 5 shared-services / BPO-style accounts

For each account, capture:
- company name
- industry
- employee-count range
- evidence of ServiceNow or mature ITSM
- evidence of after-hours or morning queue pain
- likely in-scope workflow volume signal
- one operational owner
- one executive sponsor
- one technical reviewer
- account score
- source links

## Contact map per account
Do not rely on a single contact.
Aim for **2-3 contacts per account**.

Minimum set:
- 1 operational owner
- 1 executive sponsor
- 1 technical or platform reviewer if identifiable

## Core message angles
Keep every message tied to the same three outcomes.

### Angle 1 - Queue reduction
"Start each morning with a smaller queue."

Use when:
- the account has visible backlog or SLA pressure
- the buyer owns day-to-day service desk performance

Why it works:
- it sells a business outcome, not AI novelty

### Angle 2 - Speed without extra headcount
"Take one repetitive workflow off the queue instead of asking analysts to grind through it."

Use when:
- the team looks lean
- job posts mention volume, triage, or throughput

Why it works:
- it connects directly to labor leverage without screaming replacement

### Angle 3 - Trustworthy automation
"TierZero acts only inside an approved workflow, leaves the audit trail on the ticket, and escalates exceptions cleanly."

Use when:
- the buyer is in a regulated or control-heavy environment
- security or compliance stakeholders will matter early

Why it works:
- it separates TierZero from sloppy copilot fluff

### Angle 4 - Narrow pilot, obvious expansion
"We start with password resets and unlocks because they are repetitive, measurable, and safe to audit. If the proof is real, we expand into adjacent workflows."

Use when:
- the buyer thinks the first workflow sounds small

Why it works:
- it frames the wedge as intentional, not limited ambition

## Outbound motion

Use the narrow-wedge language. Do not broaden into generic AI automation.

Use the qualifying questions in `go-to-market/FIRST_OUTBOUND_SET.md`.

### Step 5 - send the pilot package
Once interest is real, send a tight pilot package instead of dribbling assets one by one.

## What not to say in outbound
Do not lead with:
- general AI transformation
- autonomous agents replacing the service desk
- platform breadth before the first workflow lands
- generic productivity claims without queue evidence
- labor-savings math by itself

## Prospecting rule
If the account does not have obvious queue pain, repetitive identity work, and a believable sponsor, move on.

The whole wedge works because it is narrow, painful, and measurable.
That discipline matters more than a bigger list.
